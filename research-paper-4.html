The Genetic Ancestry of African Americans, Latinos, and European Americans Across the United States

Purpose

To research how population admixture affects the relationship between self-reported ancestry and genetic ancestry in different regions. Population admixture refers to when people from different continents or isolated populations interbreed. This research can potentially lead to new treatments and inform medical sciences about conditions found in certain haplogroups. This may also further research for historians and sociologists.

Methods

For their ancestry analysis the sampled group consisted of 5,269 self-reported African Americans, 8,663 Latinos, and 148,789 European Americans, who are 23andMe customers. Their geographic analysis sampled group consisted of 1,970 African Americans, 2,944 Latinos, and 45,783 European Americans. This sample is representative of the U.S. because of its reflectiveness of the U.S. 2010 census. The customers supplied saliva samples and consented to have their genetic data be used in research. The participants also answered an ancestry survey and an ethnicity survey relating to ethnic background, current zip code of residency, state of birth, and ancestry. Results

            
African Americans (please refer to figure 1 in the article) 

            

                The majority of African Americans with African ancestry are located in southern states, primarily in South Carolina and Georgia. The mean proportion of 
                African ancestry in African Americans is approximately 74.8%. However the relationship between
                 African Americans and Native American ancestry is not as profound. It is estimated that only 
                 22% of African Americans carry Native American ancestry. Oklahoma has the highest proportion 
                 of African Americans carrying Native American ancestry in the U.S. which could be due to 
                 Oklahoma being the place where these populations first interacted. This observation supports
                  the theory that ancestry is reflective of historical migration events.


            
Latinos (please refer to figure 2 in the article) 

            

                According to the data, Latinos in the U.S. on average carry approximately 65.1% European 
                ancestry, 18% Native American ancestry, and 6.2% African ancestry. Southwestern states have
                 the highest estimated levels of self-reported Latinos who carry Native American ancestry. 
                 Whereas, the highest mean levels of Latinos who carry African ancestry were living in or were 
                 born in Southern states, particularly Louisiana.


            
Europeans (please refer to figure 3 in the article) 
     
            

                Approximately 0.98% of Europeans carry African ancestry and 0.26% of Europeans carry Native 
                American ancestry. Even though the majority of Europeans don’t carry non-european ancestry, 
                the small percentage that do is still millions of individuals. The European Americans who carry
                 Native American ancestry are primarily located west of the Mississippi River. On the other 
                 hand, Europeans with African Ancestry were most likely located at cities with sea ports 
                 relating to the trans-Atlantic slave trade. African American ancestry in European Americans 
                 is strongly correlated with the proportion of population of African Americans in a state. 
                 The higher the proportion the more African ancestry in European Americans.

        


        

            
Limitations

            
One limitation of this study is that the dataset does not provide enough information to make  
                claims about Native American, nor West African ancestry. It’s important to note that this study
                 is inapplicable to mixed race people and Asian people. Another limitation is that 23andMe kits
                  are either $99 or $199 which could discourage low-income people from participating. Also the
                   majority of African Americans surveyed were living or born in California or New York which 
                   could be a result of population density. This is a limitation because African Americans have
                    a lower mean African ancestry than African Americans living in the south. Additionally, sex bias can 
                    potentially skew the results which is due to factors such as exploitation, or more 
                    European men came to the Americas than European women.
