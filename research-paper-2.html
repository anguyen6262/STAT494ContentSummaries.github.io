New approaches to population stratification in genome-wide association studies

Purpose

To review the advantages and disadvantages of methods for correcting stratification and determine the optimal method under certain conditions.

Methods

    The following are methods used to detect stratification. They use software such as PLINK, EIGENSTRAT, STRUCTURE, STRAT, ADMIXTURE, AIMS.
  • The most common approach is to compute a genomic control (lambdaGC). If lambdaGC is approximately equal to 1 then there is no stratification. Whereas a lambdaGC greater than 1 means that there is some sort of stratification. However this approach is effective for populations from recent genetic drifts, but is inaccurate for ancient populations. Genetic drift is the the process of dividing association statistics by lambda.
  • Structured Association: Accurately corrects stratification in datasets whose population structure is the only type of sample structure. The data is grouped in different subpopulations. Then the association statistics are computed. Due to the large size of genome wide datasets these studies have high computational costs. However there is always new development of software to reduce these costs which would make structured association an excellent option for inferring population structure and computing association statistics. It is important to note that this method fails to account for family structure and cryptic relatedness.
  • PCA: Primarily shows the effects of differential bias. It removes the confounding effect of when a genotype isn't associated with phenotype, but when a genotype and phenotype are both associated with ancestry. PCA creates a greater correction to data that have greater differences in allele frequencies. This makes sense because PC1 is created based on the greatest variance of the data points. This can be improved by dividing by lambdaGC, but the test statistics that account specifically for family structure will still not be accurate. This also wouldn't work if family structure in the sample could potentially produce spurious principal components. This is due to the lack of variance in genetic ancestry. A solution to this problem is to use SNP loadings from a different sample in the same study that does have family structure. This is only possible if the size of the dataset is large enough. Another limitation is that it only accounts for fixed effects and doesn't take into account the relatedness of individuals. Variance can be because people are from different population structures. PCA is still being developed and can be used. Currently the main solution to correct its limitations is to delete or not include some data.
  • Family-Based Association tests: They are immune to stratification because the alleles come from the same genetic ancestry. However the limitation is that information between families is required to calculate the test statistic. One way to fix this limitation is to transform in between family information to a rank statistic then combining the results within family information. This ensures that the tests are completely immune to stratification.
  • Mixed Models: This approach models the phenotypes as mixed, fixed or random effects. This can be used to correct for family structure with PC covariates. It's debated on whether to use fixed or random effects, random effects have proven to be effective in stratification correction. The drawback is that this method is computationally intensive.

To compare these methods they conducted two simulations for each method. The sample consisted of two different populations. The first population had 300 cases and 200 controls whereas the second population had 200 cases and 300 controls. They used 99,900 normally differentiated markers. In simulation 1 everyone was unrelated. In simulation 2, population 1 individuals were unrelated and in population 2 there were sibling pairs in the population. They also calculated lambdaGC for a few methods with software to correct false positives. They only used one principal component for methods that involved PCA because including additional principal components had negligible effect on the results.

Results

They used P-P plots to detect stratification. These are functionally the same as QQ plots, except they use log transformed p-values and plot lambdas. To determine the effectiveness of our stratification we look at the distance from the dots y=x. If everything was correct about our model then all the SNPs would be on y=x. If a point is far from y=x right away then something is wrong with our model. If points start differing towards then that model is still effective.

Conclusion

For studies where stratification is unimportant an effective method would be to use mixed models without principal component covariates and association tests are great for studies that don't involve datasets containing family structure or cryptic relatedness.

References

Price, Alkes L, et al. “New Approaches to Population Stratification in Genome-Wide Association Studies.” Nature Reviews. Genetics, vol. 11, no. 7, 2010, pp. 459–63., https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg2813.
